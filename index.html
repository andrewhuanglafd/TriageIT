Can Do?

Single-Player Mode

Solo training. Pick a deck size — Sprint (10), Standard (25), Long (50), Marathon (100), or any custom number. Beat the clock and your own accuracy. After the drill ends, you'll get a letter grade, an earned rank, and the option to review every card you missed with the full rationale.

Multiplayer Mode

Host creates a lobby and shares the 4-letter code. Players join from their phones. Same cards, same order, separate timers. Choose head-to-head or team-vs-team. Most correct wins; total time is the tiebreaker. Top 3 get gold/silver/bronze. Stay in the lobby for multiple rounds — repeat winners earn 2×, 3×, 4× WINNER badges.

⚠ Hard Mode

The deck shifts to the borderline RED-vs-BLACK judgment calls that decide who lives in a real MCI: apneic patients (does breathing return after a single head-tilt?), agonal-but-still-breathing patients who look dying but are salvageable, and shock-state patients in extremis whose RPM is profoundly red. No greens. Very few yellows. Tests the discipline of one airway intervention before moving on.

☠ Expert Mode

Sudden death. Same brutal deck as Hard Mode — borderline calls only — but one wrong answer ends your run. Miss a single tag and you're done; the game generates a randomized line-of-duty incident (skipped 360, struck by traffic, flashover, ran out of air) and the run is over. In multiplayer, dying just means you're out for the round — surviving players keep going, and you watch the leaderboard fill in. Built for the firefighter or medic who wants to know they can hold the line, every time.

Training, Not Protocol

A game cannot replace certified MCI instruction. Real-world triage involves judgment, scope of practice, and incomplete information beyond what cards can capture.
